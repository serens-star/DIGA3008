Moulthrop’s Essay & How it Speaks to the Interenet Today

Moulthrop’s essay, ‘You Say You Want a Revolution? Hypertext and the Laws of Media’ speaks directly to the evolution of the internet today, even though it was written before the modern web took shape. His essay discusses hypertext as a revolutionary medium that was expected to upend traditional information hierarchies, decentralize authority, and create a more democratic exchange of ideas. Moulthrop notes that early hypertext theorists like Ted Nelson envisioned a dynamic, open, and participatory network. However, instead of a decentralized utopia, large corporations (Yahoo, CNN, etc.) become the dominant sources of information. Today this is even more relevant with tech giants like Google, Meta, Amazon, and Apple being in charge of a large majority of people’s personal information. The internet is no longer the free, open space early pioneers imagined, but rather a tightly managed corporate landscape.

Moulthrop also discusses how hypertext was supposed to liberate readers from linear, book-like experiences and empower them to create their own pathways of knowledge. While the internet offers interconnected information, a lot of it is algorithmically curated, which limits genuine exploration. Instead of purely text-based exploration, the internet today is dominated by multimedia-video content, gaming and immersive digital experiences, Moulthrop foreshadows this shift, by mentioning MP3 swapping, multiplayer games, and instant messaging as early examples of how hypertext would evolve beyond just textual links

Moulthrop’s insights remain strikingly relevant today. The internet has followed the trajectory he predicted, because instead of breaking away from traditional power structures, it has often reinforced them. While hypertext and digital media have transformed communication, they have also been co-opted by economic and political forces. His work encourages a critical reflection on whether today’s internet is truly revolutionary or just a continuation of systems in new forms.
